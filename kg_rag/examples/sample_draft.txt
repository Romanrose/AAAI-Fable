At the edge of a walled garden, a careful caretaker wanted one quiet bed to thrive better than the rest. He first cleared the stones, opened a patch to the sun, and mixed the soil until the roots could breathe. Then he placed green frames above the bed, saying they would catch what the day offered and turn it into food the seedlings could keep.

At first nothing looked different. But after several bright mornings, the leaves in that bed thickened and stood taller than the shoots in the shade. The nearby compost heap gave off warmth, and the watered paths carried what the bed needed, yet none of those helpers could replace the sunlight or the green frames. Without both, the bed stayed pale and thin.

To settle an argument, the caretaker covered half the bed with a cloth and left the other half bare. Days later, the uncovered side had grown firm and rich, while the hidden side lagged behind. From then on, everyone in the garden understood that steady growth came from preparing the right conditions and letting the green workers turn light into stored strength.
